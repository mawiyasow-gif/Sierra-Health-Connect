Malaria Prevention

Malaria remains one of the leading causes of illness and death in Sierra Leone, particularly among children under five and pregnant women. Understanding prevention is crucial.

Key Facts

1

Malaria is transmitted through the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes, which typically bite at night.

2

Sierra Leone has one of the highest malaria burdens in the world, with transmission occurring year-round, peaking during the rainy season (May to November).

3

Children under five and pregnant women are at the highest risk of severe illness and death from malaria.

4

Early diagnosis and prompt treatment with artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs) are essential for recovery.

Prevention & Action Tips

✓ Sleep under insecticide-treated mosquito nets (ITNs) every night, all year round.
✓ Seek medical attention immediately if you or your child develops fever, chills, headache, or body aches.
✓ Pregnant women should attend all antenatal visits and take intermittent preventive treatment (IPTp) as prescribed.
✓ Eliminate standing water around your home where mosquitoes breed — cover water containers and clear blocked drains.
✓ Wear long-sleeved clothing and use mosquito repellent in the evenings.

Maternal Health

Regular antenatal care saves lives. Every pregnant woman deserves quality care before, during, and after delivery.

Key Facts

1

Attend at least 8 antenatal visits during pregnancy.

2

Take iron and folic acid supplements daily to prevent anemia.

Prevention & Action Tips

✓ Visit your nearest clinic as soon as you know you’re pregnant.
✓ Deliver at a health facility with skilled birth attendants.

Child Immunization

Vaccines protect children from life-threatening diseases. Following the national immunization schedule gives your child the best start in life.

Key Facts

1

Immunization prevents an estimated 2-3 million deaths globally every year from diseases like diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, and measles.

2

Sierra Leone's Expanded Programme on Immunization (EPI) provides free vaccines to all children under one year

3

Key vaccines include BCG (tuberculosis), OPV (polio), Pentavalent (5-in-1), measles, yellow fever, and rotavirus vaccines.

4

Children should complete all vaccinations by their first birthday, with some booster doses later

Prevention & Action Tips

✓ Follow the national immunization schedule — keep your child's vaccination card safe and bring it to every visit
✓ Vaccinate your child on time — delayed vaccinations leave your child vulnerable to preventable diseases.
✓ All government health facilities provide free immunization services
✓ If you miss a scheduled vaccination date, take your child to the nearest health facility as soon as possible.
✓ Vaccines are safe and effective — mild side effects like fever or soreness are normal and temporary

Nutrition & Healthy Living

Good nutrition is the foundation of health. A balanced diet helps prevent malnutrition, boosts immunity, and supports proper growth and development.

Key Facts

1

Malnutrition contributes to nearly half of all child deaths in Sierra Leone, often due to poor diet and lack of breastfeeding.

2

Exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months provides all nutrients babies need and protects against infections.

3

A balanced diet should include staples (rice, cassava), proteins (beans, fish, eggs), fruits, and vegetables.

4

Iron deficiency anemia is common, especially among women and children — eating iron-rich foods helps prevent this.

Prevention & Action Tips

✓ Breastfeed exclusively for the first 6 months — no water, formula, or other foods.
✓ Continue breastfeeding alongside complementary foods until age 2 or beyond.
✓ Eat a variety of foods daily — include locally available fruits and vegetables.
✓ Wash fruits and vegetables thoroughly before eating to remove dirt and harmful germs.
✓ Drink clean, treated water — boil or chlorinate water from unsafe sources.
✓ Reduce salt and sugar intake to prevent hypertension and diabetes later in life.

Disease Prevention Tips

Preventing disease starts with simple daily habits. Good hygiene, sanitation, and awareness can protect you and your family from many illnesses.

Key Facts

1

Handwashing with soap can reduce diarrheal diseases by up to 50% and respiratory infections by 25%.

2

Cholera, typhoid, and diarrhea spread through contaminated water and poor sanitation — common during rainy seasons.

3

Lassa fever is transmitted through contact with infected rodents or their urine/droppings — common in rural areas.

4

Tuberculosis (TB) spreads through the air when an infected person coughs or sneezes — early treatment prevents spread.

Prevention & Action Tips

✓ Wash hands with soap after using the toilet, before eating, and after handling animals.
✓ Use clean latrines and keep your environment clean — dispose of waste properly.
✓ Drink only treated or boiled water — avoid water from unknown sources.
✓ Cover your mouth when coughing or sneezing to prevent spreading respiratory infections.
✓ Keep food covered and eat freshly cooked meals — avoid street food with poor hygiene.
✓ Store food in sealed containers to protect from rodents and insects.
✓ Seek medical help early if you have persistent fever, cough, or diarrhea — don't wait.

Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to common health questions from our community.

Common malaria symptoms include fever, chills, headache, body aches, nausea, and vomiting. Children under 5 and pregnant women are at highest risk. If you have these symptoms, visit the nearest health facility immediately for a rapid diagnostic test. Early treatment with ACTs is critical.

Free healthcare is available for children under 5, pregnant women, and lactating mothers at all government hospitals and clinics under the Free Health Care Initiative. Use our "Health Services" directory to find the nearest facility in your district.

Your child needs vaccines at birth, 6 weeks, 10 weeks, 14 weeks, 9 months, and 18 months. These protect against TB, Polio, Diphtheria, Tetanus, Hepatitis B, Pneumonia, Diarrhea, Measles, and Yellow Fever. Visit your nearest clinic with your child’s health card for the full schedule.

Prevent cholera by drinking only treated/boiled water, washing hands with soap after using toilet and before eating, keeping food covered, and using clean latrines. During outbreaks, avoid raw foods and crowded areas with poor sanitation.

For emergencies like heavy bleeding, difficulty breathing, unconsciousness, or severe injury: 1. Call your nearest hospital ambulance immediately. 2. Keep the person lying down and warm. 3. Do not give food or water if unconscious. Check our "Emergency Contacts" page for numbers in your area.

Some herbs can be harmful during pregnancy and may cause miscarriage or birth defects. Always consult a nurse, midwife, or doctor before taking any herbal remedy, traditional medicine, or new supplement while pregnant.
